Research Profile

Dimensional ontology, quantum metrology, and unified frameworks for physical constants.

Overview

Bourassa’s current work develops the Quantized Dimensional Ledger (QDL), a unified 3L + 2F length–frequency basis intended to bring algebraic closure and conceptual clarity to mechanical, electromagnetic, gravitational, and thermodynamic quantities. Within this framework, mass and time are treated as derived constructs, while the Quantum Measurement Unit (QMU) provides a common dimensional reference cell.

His research focuses on:

  • Constructing a 100-entry QMU ledger with cross-domain dimensional closure.
  • Classifying fundamental constants into invariants, couplings, and emergent ratios.
  • Exploring scalar–tensor extensions of Einstein–Maxwell theory aligned with QDL.
  • Designing laboratory-scale tests (torsion balances, NV centers) sensitive to QDL effects.

Canonical QDL Papers

Core references that define the Quantized Dimensional Ledger framework, its ledger-lattice foundations, and key applications.

Quantized Dimensional Ledger as a Unified Dimensional Closure Framework for the Standard Model and Gravity

Bourassa, J. D. (2025). Zenodo. DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.17742903

Develops a unified QDL closure rule for Standard Model fields and a scalar–tensor gravity sector within a common 3L + 2F ledger. A Quantized Dimensional Cell acts as the organizing structure for fields, couplings, and constants, providing the main theoretical backbone for applying dimensional closure across particle physics and gravitation.

Dimensional Closure and Ledger Lattices in Effective Field Theories

Bourassa, J. D. (2025). Zenodo. DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.17773324

Introduces the five-dimensional integer ledger lattice, the Quantized Dimensional Cell, and the unimodular-invariant closure criterion for effective field theories. This foundational paper formalizes the QDL ledger structure and closure condition used in later applications to SMEFT and other field-theoretic frameworks.

Ledger-Closure Constraints on the SMEFT: A Lattice-Theoretic Derivation of Operator Exclusions and Wilson-Coefficient Relations

Bourassa, J. D. (2025). Zenodo. DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.17780443

Applies the QDL ledger-lattice framework to the Standard Model Effective Field Theory (SMEFT) through dimension eight. By encoding operators as ledger vectors and enforcing the closure condition, the paper excludes entire families of four-fermion contact operators as fundamental terms and derives algebraic relations among Wilson coefficients, providing a structural constraint principle for SMEFT.

The Quantized Dimensional Ledger for Metrology: Dimensional Closure, QMU Ledgers, and the Ontology of Physical Constants

Bourassa, J. D. (2025). Zenodo. DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.17619526

Establishes the QDL metrology ledger and a 100-entry Quantum Measurement Unit (QMU) table spanning mechanics, electromagnetism, gravitation, and thermodynamics. It develops a dimensional ontology for physical constants, separating them into dimensionless invariants, dimensional couplings, and emergent geometric–frequency ratios, and provides machine-readable ledgers suitable for independent audit.

An earlier, broader preprint, A Quantized Dimensional Ledger Framework for Gravitation, Electromagnetism, and Metrology (Zenodo DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.17515009 ), introduced the initial QDL concepts and has been superseded in scope and detail by the unified framework and metrology ledger papers above.

For Journal Editors & Collaborators

Notes on scope, reproducibility, and how the QDL work fits within theoretical and applied physics.

Scope & Reproducibility

The QDL program is intended as a dimensional ontology and metrological framework rather than a competing dynamical theory. The emphasis is on:

  • Algebraic closure across mechanical, electromagnetic, and gravitational quantities.
  • Ledger-style documentation of exponents and units for transparent audit.
  • Clear separation of dimensional structure from specific field equations.

All ledger data supporting recent manuscripts are publicly available via Zenodo, with machine-readable CSV files and explanatory README documentation to facilitate independent analysis, replication, and critical review.
