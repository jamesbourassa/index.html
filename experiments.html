Program overview

From closure rules to tabletop tests: how QDL maps a ledger grammar onto measurable signatures.

Dimensional closure as an experimental guide

In QDL, every field, coupling, and observable is represented as a 5-component integer ledger vector in a 3L + 2F basis. Interactions and measurement relations must close onto an integer multiple of the Quantized Dimensional Cell. When this closure principle is applied to experimental observables, it yields:

  • Testable scaling predictions for how measured quantities vary with geometry and control parameters.
  • Constraints on which combinations of constants can appear in empirical fits.
  • A structured “admissibility check” that can reject relations even when unit-balance appears satisfied.

The experimental program is therefore a direct test of the QDL admissibility layer: if ledger-based closures and scalings fail systematically, the framework is falsified. If they are supported, the ledger structure gains empirical weight and motivates deeper theoretical integration.

Torsion-balance experiments

Ledger-based torque and deflection scaling vs. arm length, mass distribution, and environmental fields.

Concept

Precision torsion balances are highly sensitive to small forces and are a natural platform for testing QDL scaling laws. In the ledger picture, torque, deflection angle, arm length, and test-mass configuration are all assigned ledger vectors, and the net dimensional content must close onto the QDC when combined with the relevant fields and constants.

QDL-style closure implies testable exponent relations among:

  • Arm length and test mass geometry.
  • Restoring torque and angular deflection.
  • Gravitational and non-gravitational contributions.

Deviations from conventional scaling—when analyzed in a ledger-consistent way—would indicate a closure/admissibility structure beyond standard dimensional expectations.

Status and next steps

The QDL Experimental Validation Protocol outlines torsion-balance setups in which QDL and standard theory give distinguishable length- and mass-scaling predictions. Experimental collaborations are invited to:

  • Map existing torsion datasets into a 3L + 2F ledger representation.
  • Design new runs with systematic variation of arm length, mass distribution, and shielding.
  • Compare fit quality and residuals for QDL vs. standard scaling assumptions.

Reference: QDL Experimental Validation Protocol – 10.5281/zenodo.17654442

NV-center frequency shifts

Coherence signatures and spin-resonance constraints in solid-state defects.

Concept

Nitrogen–vacancy (NV) centers in diamond provide a precise spin-resonance probe that can be engineered into well-controlled environments. QDL expects that certain combinations of applied fields, geometry, and constants may exhibit ledger-coherent frequency shifts tied to the 3L + 2F structure.

  • The observed shift is expressed as a function of ledger vectors for fields, constants, and geometry.
  • Only combinations consistent with the QDC are admissible.
  • Exponent patterns can be tested against high-precision NV data across controlled parameter sweeps.
Status and next steps

The Coherent Ledger Fields paper outlines NV-center scenarios where QDL coherence requirements may produce detectable shifts or patterns not captured by conventional parameterizations.

  • Start by reanalyzing existing NV datasets in ledger form.
  • Design runs that vary geometry and field configurations to isolate QDL-specific exponent signatures.

Reference: Coherent Ledger Fields – 10.5281/zenodo.17803804

Cavity length–frequency scaling

Teaching-lab and precision setups as L–F ledger probes.

Concept

Cavity resonators offer a clean environment in which length and frequency are tightly linked. In the QDL framework, cavity length and resonant frequency are expressed in a length–frequency basis, and their relation is tested against ledger-admissible forms.

  • Vary cavity geometry and materials while tracking ledger-based invariants.
  • Compare standard fits to QDL-inspired ledger fits across geometry sweeps.
  • Use high-Q setups to probe small deviations in scaling behavior.
Status and next steps

Existing undergraduate and graduate cavity experiments can be repurposed as low-barrier tests of QDL scaling by rewriting the analysis in an L–F basis and comparing residual structure.

  • Recast the lab pipeline as a ledger-auditable measurement chain.
  • Run geometry sweeps designed to separate standard vs. ledger-admissible parameterizations.

Reference: Teaching Dimensional Analysis with a Length–Frequency Basis – 10.5281/zenodo.17663340

Metamaterial coherence & dispersion

Engineered media as controlled environments for testing ledger-structured scaling.

Concept

Metamaterials can be engineered to exhibit exotic dispersion and tailored field configurations. QDL treats these as candidate environments where ledger-consistent combinations of effective parameters may “lock” together if dimensional closure plays an organizing role in nature.

  • Effective permittivity, permeability, and geometry are treated as ledger quantities.
  • Closure criteria predict which combinations are admissible or resonance-stable.

Benchmarks & null tests

Direct data confrontation: QDL predictions vs baseline models using public datasets and reanalyzed measurements.

What “benchmark” means here

Benchmarks are explicit, falsifiable comparisons between QDL-admissible scaling relations and standard dimensional or EFT parameterizations. Each benchmark is designed to be auditable: dataset provenance, model forms, fit controls, and residuals are published alongside the analysis.

  • Binary or near-binary outcomes where possible (pass / fail).
  • No hidden tuning: all adjustable parameters are declared.
  • Ledger-form transparency: intermediate quantities expressed in 3L + 2F form.
  • Reproducibility: CSV tables + scripts/notebooks + plotted residuals.

The goal is to make it easy for an external group to replicate (or refute) each claim using the same dataset and the same declared analysis choices.

Published benchmark (executed)

Benchmark 1 is live: torsion-balance limits analyzed with residual-first diagnostics.

The first QDL benchmark has been executed using published Eöt-Wash torsion-balance limits on Yukawa-type deviations from gravity. The analysis compares a flexible baseline fit against a constrained, ledger-motivated comparison family, with residual plots as the primary diagnostic.

  • Public dataset digitized from Eöt-Wash results
  • Declared model freedom (no hidden tuning)
  • Residual-first reporting (structured residuals yes/no)
  • Outcome: null confirmation (no additional structured residuals introduced)

Reference:

Plain-English takeaway: the framework survives an initial, auditable null test on a leading precision dataset without introducing artifacts or hidden tuning. Future benchmarks must be independently discriminating to establish added explanatory power.
