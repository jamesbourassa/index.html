Experimental validation program

QDL Experimental Validation Protocol

The QDL Experimental Validation Protocol outlines how a conserved 3L + 2F Quantized Dimensional Cell could leave measurable signatures in precision tabletop experiments. The goal is to design tests where conventional theories and the Quantized Dimensional Ledger make distinguishable scaling predictions, allowing QDL to be evaluated as a falsifiable framework.

1. Precision torsion-balance scaling

Torsion balances provide an exquisitely sensitive probe of force and torque scaling. In the QDL program, structured changes in arm length, mass distribution, and geometry are used to isolate ledger-driven scaling relations.

  • Vary arm length and mass separation while keeping conventional parameters fixed.
  • Look for systematic deviations in torque vs. geometry predicted by QDL closure.
  • Exploit high-quality factors and low-noise environments to resolve small effects.

2. NV-center frequency-shift measurements

Nitrogen–vacancy (NV) centers in diamond offer a well-characterized spin-resonance platform. QDL-motivated field configurations are used to test whether the ledger structure implies structured offsets in resonance frequencies.

  • Apply controlled magnetic and electric field configurations tied to L–F scaling.
  • Compare measured resonance shifts against standard spin Hamiltonians.
  • Search for reproducible departures consistent with QDL dimensional closure.

3. Cavity-mode length–frequency scaling

Resonant cavities convert geometry directly into frequency spectra. QDL predicts specific scaling patterns in mode structure when cavity dimensions are varied in ways that respect or violate the underlying 3L + 2F cell.

  • Systematically scale cavity length and aspect ratio while tracking resonance modes.
  • Test for scaling collapses or splits that align with QDL ledger predictions.
  • Use high-stability oscillators and precise metrology to control frequency error bars.

4. Metamaterial dispersion-collapse tests

Engineered metamaterials can be designed to approximate target dispersion relations. QDL suggests that certain “ledger-aligned” structures may exhibit dispersion collapses or coherence patterns that differ from standard effective-medium models.

  • Design metamaterial lattices to approximate QDC-inspired dimensional structures.
  • Measure dispersion curves and effective indices across frequency bands.
  • Look for organized departures from conventional models matching QDL scaling.
