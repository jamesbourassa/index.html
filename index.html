The Quantized Dimensional Ledger (QDL) replaces conventional MLTQ dimensional analysis with a two-base L–F basis:

Length (L) Frequency (F) Mass derived: \(m = hF/c^2\) Charge as rotational phase

QDL enforces a conserved ledger cell with dimensional content \(L^{3}F^{2}\). We refer to the quantized \(3L + 2F\) geometric volume element as the Quantized Dimensional Cell (QDC), which serves as the fundamental structural unit for field and geometric configurations.

Core cell identity:
  \( [\text{QDC}] = L^{3}F^{2} \)

Example mappings:
  Energy density: \( [E] \sim L^{3}F^{2} \)
  Curvature: \( [\mathcal{R}] = L^{-2} \)
  Velocity: \( [v] = LF \)
  Mass: \( [m] = L^{-1}F^{-1} \) (in QDL ledger units)

All physical quantities are expressed as integer-exponent ledger entries in L and F, with QDC providing the closure constraint.

Recent work develops a scalar–tensor action in which QDC coherence is tied to two coupling functions \(Y(\sigma)\) and \(Z(\sigma)\), providing a path from dimensional analysis to effective field descriptions of gravity and electromagnetism.

Key idea: instead of tracking units in MLTQ-space, QDL uses integer-ledger combinations in a 3L + 2F basis. The preserved QDC cell becomes the organizing geometry behind constants, fields, and experimental scaling laws.

Experimental Roadmap

QDL-derived tests across torsion, spin systems, cavities, and metamaterials.

Protocol DOI

The QDL Experimental Validation Protocol outlines four primary experimental families designed to stress-test the ledger and QDC structure:

  • Torsion-balance scaling: searches for anomalous torque signatures under geometric scaling, consistent with QDL’s treatment of gravitation and curvature-ledger relations.
  • NV-center frequency shifts: probes for subtle frequency offsets in spin defects under engineered field configurations motivated by QDL corrections.
  • Cavity scaling collapse: tests whether resonances respect QDL’s L–F ledger scaling, particularly in inverse-length cavity experiments.
  • Metamaterial resonances: explores engineered structures that mimic QDC-like coherence and investigate potential deviations from standard EM expectations.
Each of these platforms is designed to be falsifiable in the Popper sense: QDL makes explicit claims about how frequency, length, and curvature must scale if the ledger model is correct.
Torsion experiments

Looks for systematic deviations in torque scaling as arm length, mass distribution, and environmental fields are varied, using QDL’s \(L^{3}F^{2}\) closure as the organizing constraint.

Spin and NV systems

Uses spin defects and precision frequency readout to search for QDC-motivated offset terms, potentially distinguishing QDL corrections from standard spin–orbit and Zeeman contributions.

Cavity and metamaterial tests

Applies QDL’s ledger to resonant structures, asking whether measured mode patterns and scaling relationships align with standard EM or hint at additional QDC-structured degrees of freedom.

Institute & Contact

Independent research program, publishing via Zenodo and peer-reviewed journals.

The QDL Physics Institute is an independent research effort run by James D. Bourassa, focused on:

  • Developing the QDL / QDC framework for gravitation, electromagnetism, and metrology.
  • Publishing formal derivations, experimental protocols, and teaching resources in open-access form via Zenodo and traditional journals.
  • Building a fully auditable ledger for constants, units, and dimensional structure, tied to real experimental proposals.

The institute is currently based in Huntley, Illinois (USA), with a focus on rigorous, falsifiable claims rather than speculative numerology. All core technical artifacts, including code and dimensional tables, are intended to be archived with DOIs.
