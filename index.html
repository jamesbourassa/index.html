Framework

Unified framework DOI

The Quantized Dimensional Ledger (QDL) replaces conventional MLTQ dimensional analysis with a two-base L–F basis:

Length (L) Frequency (F) Mass derived: \(m = hF/c^2\) Charge as rotational phase

Instead of treating mass, charge, and time as primitive, QDL expresses them as ledger entries in the L–F basis. A conserved, quantized ledger cell – the Quantized Dimensional Cell (QDC) – provides a common dimensional target for gravitation, electromagnetism, and metrology:

QDC structure:
  [\text{QDC}] = L^{3}F^{2}

Example mappings (ledger units):
  [g] = L^{-1}F^{-2} (acceleration)
  [\Phi_G] = L^{2}F^{2} (gravitational potential)
  [\mathcal{L}] = L^{3}F^{2} (action density)

Fields, couplings, and curvature are assembled so that the full ledger closes into the QDC. This gives a dimensional origin for scalar–tensor gravity, EM structure, and constant ledgers.

Across the current QDL manuscripts, the framework:

  • Unifies gravitation, electromagnetism, and metrology in a single integer-exponent ledger.
  • Constrains which scalar–tensor actions and EM couplings are dimensionally admissible under QDC closure.
  • Reinterprets constants (G, h, c, e, etc.) as entries in a Quantized Measurement Unit (QMU) ledger with structural relationships.
  • Generates concrete, falsifiable predictions for torsion balances, NV-centers, cavities, and metamaterials.
Why QDL matters: rather than adding a new model on top of existing physics, QDL proposes a dimensional closure rule that existing and candidate theories must obey. If the ledger principle is correct, it offers a compact organizing foundation for field theory, gravitation, and metrology.

Experimental Roadmap

Ledger-derived, falsifiable tests across torsion, spin systems, cavities, and metamaterials.

Protocol DOI

The QDL Experimental Validation Protocol outlines four primary experimental families designed to stress-test the QDL/QDC structure in independent ways:

  • Torsion-balance scaling: searches for anomalous torque signatures under geometric scaling of arm length and mass distribution, using QDL curvature–ledger relations as a guide.
  • NV-center frequency shifts: probes for subtle frequency offsets in spin defects under engineered field configurations motivated by QDL corrections to standard spin and EM environments.
  • Cavity scaling collapse: tests whether resonances respect QDL’s L–F ledger scaling in inverse-length cavity experiments, complementing the teaching cavity-lab paper.
  • Metamaterial resonances: explores engineered structures that mimic QDC-like coherence and investigate potential departures from standard EM in dispersion and mode structure.
Each platform is intended to be falsifiable in the Popper sense: QDL makes explicit claims about how length, frequency, and curvature must scale. Experimental disagreement directly constrains or rules out sectors of the QDL framework.
Torsion experiments

Searches for scaling anomalies in torsion-balance torque as geometry and environmental conditions are varied, looking for signatures consistent with QDC-governed gravitational structure.

Spin and NV systems

Uses spin defects and precision spectroscopy to search for ledger-motivated frequency offsets that cannot be accounted for by standard spin–orbit, Zeeman, or lattice effects.

Cavity and metamaterial tests

Applies QDL scaling to resonant and metamaterial structures, asking whether measured mode patterns align strictly with standard EM or hint at additional QDC-structured degrees of freedom.

Institute & Contact

Independent research at the intersection of dimensional analysis, metrology, and field theory.

The QDL Physics Institute is an independent research effort led by James D. Bourassa. The institute focuses on:

  • Developing the QDL / QDC framework for gravitation, electromagnetism, and precision metrology through unified dimensional closure.
  • Publishing formal derivations, experimental protocols, and teaching resources in open-access form via Zenodo and peer-reviewed journals.
  • Building an auditable ledger for constants, units, and dimensional structure tied directly to experimental testbeds and reproducible calculations.

The institute is based in Huntley, Illinois (USA) and is committed to rigorous, falsifiable claims rather than numerological fitting. Core technical artifacts, including ledgers, tables, and supporting materials, are archived with DOIs for long-term verification and reuse.
